Generally, the Jet engine is also known as a gas turbine engine is a reaction engine that discharges a fast-moving stable jet that generates thrust by jet propulsion and the turbine placed upstream of the hot jet flow (static pressure and energy-rich combustion chamber outlet gases) will extract the energy from the exhaust jet to run multi-staged rotors assembled to the same rotating central shaft, in which the turbine assembly is attached on to. Which transmits the energy from the turbine to the axial compressor multistage rotors, is located in a certain required number of stages (selection of “n’’ is flexible through the static pressure requirement of the whole engine design) alternatively in between the multi-stage stator assemblies, all together in a converging annulus volume leading from the very upstream of the incoming airflow after diffuser till the very inlet of the combustion chamber housing units. In the most common design, around the circumferential void volume leads from the outlet of the axial compressor housing and the inlet of turbine housing unit, which is around the circumferential volume of the central shaft, is all bolted together by metal seals sandwiched in between the bolted joints, there are multiple numbers of combustion chamber units set in between axial compressor outlet and the turbine inlet.

The combustion chamber is designed in such a way to mix the fuel and the compressed static pressure and heat-rich air for efficient sustained combustion, equipped with a spark plug of a continuous electric arc formed in between the two electrodes placed inside the combustion chamber. The fuel nozzle unit is placed inside the combustion chamber, very behind the electric arc forming electrodes of the continuous spark plug, to obtain a continuous atomized flow of aviation fuel with required fuel pressure directly onto the continuous electric arc.

The fuel injector is connected to a fuel manifold which is connected with a fuel reservoir and a fuel pump, which constantly pumps the fuel with the required fuel pressure. The combusted hot energized gas jet streams are entered into the turbine housing which comprises one or two stages of the turbine cycle with an annular set of nozzle blades and the turbine rotor reaction blades, which will extract energy from already energized gas streams expanding from the combustion chamber.

At the nozzle of the jet engine, the exhaust gas streams after the turbine will expand into exponentially higher velocity jet streams, as the reaction to the action triggered by these high-velocity jet streams is the output created by the gas turbine engines, generally known as thrust.

In aeronautical engineering, the overall pressure ratio, or overall compression ratio, is the ratio of the stagnation pressure as measured at the front and rear of the compression of a gas turbine engine. The terms compression ratio and pressure ratio are interchangeable, overall compression ratio also means the overall cycle pressure ratio which includes intake ram. A higher overall pressure ratio implies higher efficiency, but the engine will usually weigh more, so there is a compromise between the weight of the engine and the pressure ratio. A higher overall pressure ratio permitted a larger area nozzle to be fitted on the jet engine. This means that more of the heat energy is converted to jet-speed, and energetic efficiency improves. This is reflected in improvements in the engine’s specific fuel consumption.

Fuel efficiency and power-to-weight ratio are important factors to be considered while designing any fuel-consuming engine or system. High fuel-efficient systems with less weight are more efficient in extracting high outputs.in the case of aircraft and industrial jet engines, the operational cost and power output are equally important, thus there stands a need for an engine, which can extract the required power output with limited fuel consumption and less weight compared to the conventional engines.

Axial flow compressors are integral to the design of large gas turbines such as jet engines, high-speed ship engines, and small-scale power stations. They are also used in industrial applications such as large volume air separation plants, blast furnace air, fluid catalytic cracking air, and propane dehydrogenations. Due to high performance, high reliability, and flexible operation during the flight envelope, they are also used widely in aerospace engines.

The function of a compressor is generally to raise the pressure of a specified mass flow of a gas by a prescribed amount using the minimum power output. The axial compressor consists of rotating and stationary components. A shaft drives a central drum which is retained by bearings inside of a stationary tubular casing. Between the drum and the casing are rows of airfoils, and each row is connected to either the drum or the casing in an alternating manner. A pair of one row of rotating airfoils and the next row of stationary airfoils is called one stage. The rotating airfoils, also known as blades or rotors, accelerate the fluids in both the axial and circumferential directions. The stationary airfoils, also known as vanes or stators, convert the increased kinetic energy into static pressure through diffusion and redirect the flow direction of the fluid to prepare it for the rotor blades of the next stage. Pressure rises are obtained through successive stages of diffusing passages with a consequent reduction in velocity. The cross-sectional area between the rotor drum and casing is reduced in the flow direction to maintain an optimum Mach number axial velocity as the fluid is compressed. Axial flow compressors produce a continuous flow of compressed gas and have the benefits of high efficiency and large mass flow rate, particularly in relation to their size and cross-section. They do, however, require several rows of airfoils to achieve a large pressure rise, making them complex and expensive, relative to other designs (e.g., centrifugal compressors). this all shows an inevitable need for an updated and more appropriate design for the axial compressor to reduce the number of stages and to achieve a more efficient pressure rise from the minimum turbine energy transmitted through the shaft.
The compressor consumes over 55-60% of the total power generated by the gas turbine. therefore, compressor problems, such as fouling, can have a significant impact on turbine performance. Pressure ratios have increased from about 7:1 in the 1950s to 35:1 in the 2000s. the safe operating range of the compressor, at a constant speed, stretches from the surge line at the low flow end of the compressor constant speed line to the choke point at the high flow end. This operational range difference can be about 2.5-3.5 % of the design flow. The lower pressure compressor has a larger operational range than the higher-pressure compressor. therefore, the higher-pressure ratio compressors are more susceptible to fouling, and this can result in surge problems or blade excitation problems, which can lead to blade failures. A drop in pressure ratio at the turbine inlet due to filter fouling results in a substantial loss in the turbine’s overall efficiency and power produced. Hence the reduction of many blades and stages without compromising the output pressure ratio is safer.

In industrial gas turbine engines, there are trends towards using water injection at the inlet or between compressor sections, which is likely to affect airfoil life due to erosion effects. This clearly shows the need for a heat transfer management process within the axial compressor as its integral part.

The trend in axial flow compressors towards fewer airfoils(30-35 first stage blades as compared with 40 earlier), thinner blades(thickness to chord ratio has decreased from 0.1 to 0.08), bigger airfoil diameter(increased from 5-6 ft to 8-10 ft), and use of three dimensional and controlled-diffusion-airfoil blades(3D/CDA).but still, this trend forces to increase the number of stages, which will increase three weight and complexity of jet engines.in turn, this will increase the gross weight and complexity of operation for both industrial and aircraft jet engines. The blades have similar clearances and higher loading per stage (pressure ratio per stage, having gone from 1.14 to 1.18). performance of the axial compressor is sensitive to the condition of the rotor blades. Dirt on blades, due to poor filtration and maintenance practices, can be a significant source of performance deterioration. Compressor deterioration in turbine performance is indicated by slower engine acceleration, engine compressor surge or stall, lower power output, loss of engine compressor discharge pressure, and increase in compressor discharge temperature. thus, reducing the number of rotor blades and stator blades(reduction in blade loading) is more appropriate if a proper solution is applied for achieving competent pressure rise with’ ‘lesser number ‘of blades and stages. thus, there is a need to reduce the number of blades and stages without affecting the required pressure ratio. There is also a need to attain the required static pressure rise from the incoming axial flow with a lesser number of stages and the number of blades of both stator’s annular rows and rotor’s annular rows in an axial compressor assembly. It can be clearly understood that to keep the required thrust constant or a bit higher than usual, it is possible to keep the mass flow rate low, by keeping the operational RPM of the engine low. Therefore, increasing the jet velocity substantially, by allowing static pressure to be expanded at the nozzle, altogether will help in improving the aerodynamic efficiency of the flow over rotor and stator blades, combustion efficiency, turbine efficiency, and eventually the fuel efficiency of the jet engine as a whole.

The principal objective of the present invention is to attain the required static pressure rise from the incoming axial flow with a lesser number of stages and a lesser number of blades in an axial flow compressor assembly. This will reduce the TSFC (thrust specific fuel consumption) by extracting the required pressure ratio from the incoming axial flow and an increase in power to weight ratio, due to the reduction in weight and dimensions. Thus, TSFC will decrease substantially, and will in turn help to reduce the complexity and cost of maintenance of the axial compressor parts like rows of stator and rotor blades.

One or more shortcomings of conventional systems are overcome and additional advantages are provided through the provision of a system disclosed in the present disclosure. Additional features and advantages are realized through the techniques of the present disclosure.

A stator is disclosed, which is adapted to redirect the flow of a fluid in an axial flow compressor. It comprises a plurality of elongated structures, with curved contours along with an elongated geometry and two vertices. Cantilever support is physically coupled to a surface of the structure at the elongated geometry. An outer casing is physically coupled to the cantilever support, wherein the elongated structure is aligned such that a length of the elongated structure, which is defined as the distance between two vertices, is substantially parallel to a width of the adjacent stator blades in the same row of the particular stage attached to the outer casing. In an embodiment, the elongated structure may be asymmetric or symmetric and is formed by physically coupling two conical structures at a circular base of the conical structure. The cantilever support is physically coupled to a surface of the elongated structure at the elongated geometry about a center of a central axis passing through the vertices of the elongated structure.

A plurality of blades is provided, extending from the outer casing. The stator further comprises an inner casing, wherein the blades extending from the outer casing are physically coupled to the inner casing or not coupled to the inner casing, wherein the elongated structures are placed between the blades, such that the length of the elongated structure is substantially parallel to a width of the blade. The distance between the central axis of the elongated structure is lesser than the height of the blades, and the length of the elongated structure is equal to or slightly lesser than the width of the blade.

The present invention helps to reduce the blade loading, as a result, the required pressure rise can be attained by using the volume in between blades as pressure generators by making use of the design and process of the present invention, which will use the ram pressure due to forward movement of aircraft as a bonus in case of aircraft jet engines to generate more static pressure. The ratio of the work done to the peripheral velocity of rotor blade squared is the blade loading coefficient, which is a dimensionless parameter, usually blade loading is assessed by the diffusion factor, blade loading coefficient should be high enough for an axial compressor, and by employing the present invention, enough can be extracted.

The present invention highly contributes towards the solution for unsteady, non-uniform axial flow (radial direction of the compressor, reduced work done factor due to non-uniform axial flow (radial) and due to a greater number of stages), but the present invention helps to make the axial flow (radial) steady and uniform. It further helps to reduce the number of stages without the compromise of required static pressure. This results in high work done factor, which for an axial compressor stage is an empirical factor introduced to account for non-uniformity of the velocity profile in the blade passages.

The present invention further deals with the generation of adverse pressure gradient due to the increase in static pressure rise through the axial flow direction. This is a major threat prevented by contributing the first portion of the invention, that is the hollow/solid double cones in the blade passage with an axial flow acceleration phenomenon at the first portion by decreasing the cross-sectional area of the blade passage initially, according to the Bernoulli’s mass flowrate equation, when the cross-sectional area increases, the velocity of the flow increases. The elongated structures in between the blade passage will help in generating a laminar overall flow with a turbulent boundary layered flow in between the blade and the elongated structures overlapping boundary layer through blade passage also prevent boundary layer separation, adverse pressure gradient, and its negative effects and the major aerodynamic losses. The rich laminar flow will be directed perfectly towards the rotor blades, and will also prevent the boundary layer separation generation tendency on the rotor blade surfaces and in the gap in between the rotor blades. Turbulent boundary layers tend to be able to sustain an adverse pressure gradient better than an equivalent laminar boundary layer.

The cantilever support is physically coupled to the elongated structure about the center of the central axis passing through vertices of the elongated structure and is physically coupled to the outer casing from its other end, thereby providing strong support to the elongated structure.
